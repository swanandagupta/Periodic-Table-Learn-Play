LET'S LEARN ABOUT EACH GROUP OF THE PERIODIC TABLE

Group 1: Alkali Metals
Highly reactive, especially with water. One valence electron leading to the formation of +1 ions. Softness and low density. Low melting and boiling points. Characteristic flame colors (e.g., sodium gives a yellow flame).
Group 2: Alkaline Earth Metals
Less reactive than alkali metals but still reactive. Two valence electrons leading to the formation of +2 ions. Higher melting points than alkali metals. Harder and denser than alkali metals. React with water (less vigorously than Group 1).
Group 3: Scandium Group
Transition metals with +3 oxidation states. Light and strong, with applications in aerospace (scandium). Yttrium is used in electronics and lasers. Lanthanum is part of the rare earth elements and is used in camera lenses and battery electrodes.
Group 4: Titanium Group
Strong, low-density metals, with high corrosion resistance. Used in aerospace, medical implants (titanium), and nuclear reactors (zirconium). Hafnium is used in control rods for nuclear reactors due to its neutron absorption properties.
Group 5: Vanadium Group
Transition metals with multiple oxidation states, often +5. Vanadium is used in steel alloys for strength. Niobium and tantalum are used in superconductors and electronics.
Group 6: Chromium Group
High melting points and hardness. Form oxides with varying oxidation states. Chromium adds corrosion resistance and a shiny finish in alloys (e.g., stainless steel). Molybdenum and tungsten are used in high-strength steel and industrial applications requiring high temperatures.
Group 7: Manganese Group
Transition metals with multiple oxidation states, often +2, +4, +7. Manganese is crucial in steelmaking, adding strength and hardness. Technetium is radioactive and used in medical imaging.
Group 8: Iron Group
Essential transition metals with common oxidation states of +2 and +3. High tensile strength and density. Iron is the most widely used metal, primarily in steel production. Osmium and ruthenium are rare and used in specialized industrial applications.
Group 9: Cobalt Group
Transition metals with multiple oxidation states. Hard and brittle, with high melting points. Magnetic properties, especially cobalt. Used in alloys, magnets (cobalt), and as catalysts in chemical reactions.
Group 10: Nickel Group
Transition metals with variable oxidation states, often +2 or +4. Good catalysts in industrial processes. High melting points and density. Corrosion-resistant and used in alloys (e.g., stainless steel) and coins.
Group 11: Coinage Metals
Includes copper, silver, and gold. Known for their excellent conductivity and resistance to corrosion. Traditionally used in coins and jewelry. Silver is also used in electronics and solar panels.
Group 12: Zinc Group
Includes zinc, cadmium, and mercury. Often used in protective coatings (zinc plating). Mercury is the only metal that is liquid at room temperature. Cadmium has uses in batteries and pigments.
Group 13: Boron Group
Includes boron, aluminum, gallium, indium, and thallium. Boron is used in glass and ceramics. Aluminum is lightweight and used in packaging, construction, and transportation. Gallium is used in electronics and solar panels.
Group 14: Carbon Group
Includes carbon, silicon, germanium, tin, and lead. Carbon is the basis of organic life. Silicon is used in electronics and solar panels. Tin and lead are used in various alloys and solders.
Group 15: Nitrogen Group
Includes nitrogen, phosphorus, arsenic, antimony, and bismuth. Nitrogen is essential for life, used in fertilizers. Phosphorus is crucial in biological molecules. Arsenic, antimony, and bismuth have various industrial uses.
Group 16: Oxygen Group
Includes oxygen, sulfur, selenium, tellurium, and polonium. Oxygen is vital for respiration. Sulfur is used in fertilizers and chemicals. Selenium and tellurium are used in electronics and solar cells.
Group 17: Halogens
Highly reactive nonmetals. Includes fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine, and astatine. Form salts when combined with metals. Used in disinfectants, dyes, and various industrial applications.
Group 18: Noble Gases
Includes helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, and radon. Inert gases with very low reactivity. Used in lighting, welding, and as inert atmospheres for chemical reactions. Helium is used in balloons and cryogenics.
Lanthanides
Rare earth elements including lanthanum, cerium, neodymium, and others. Used in magnets, phosphors, and catalysts. Have similar properties, including high magnetic susceptibility and catalytic activity.
Actinides
Includes actinium, thorium, uranium, and others. Radioactive elements, including those used in nuclear reactors and weapons. Uranium and plutonium are used in nuclear energy and weapons.
